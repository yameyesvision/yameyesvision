### Story 1 — The First Time He Called Me Dad

For almost the first year and a half of knowing my son, he didn't know I was his dad.

I wasn't going to rush it. I wasn't going to put that on him before he was ready. I wanted it to be real. Organic. His.

So I just showed up. Every time. And I let the relationship build on its own terms.

Then one day, it happened.

I was joking with him about getting Apple Jacks when I knew he wanted Froot Loops. He looked at me dead serious, put his iPad down, and said: *"Dad, I don't want Apple Jacks. Make me some Froot Loops."*

He probably said it like it was the most natural thing in the world. For him, maybe it was.

For me, it was everything.

In five years, I've heard it maybe fifteen times. I've never pushed for it. But every single time it comes out — it puts a smile on my heart that nothing else can match.

Because it was real. It wasn't forced. It came from him.

That's what words do when they're genuine. They land in a way that stays.

There's a difference between something said because it's expected and something said because it's true. You can feel that difference when it hits. And when it does — it doesn't leave.

A lot of us, especially men, think presence is enough. Show up and they'll know. Provide and they'll feel it. But sometimes love needs a word. A name. Five words over a bowl of cereal.

I wrote a whole chapter about this in my book. About how love left unspoken becomes regret. About why words matter more than we let ourselves believe.

It wasn't a lecture I wrote to my son. It was a reminder — because I needed it too.

Say the true things. Say them when they're ready. And when they come, let them mean everything they're supposed to mean.

### Story 2 — I Bought a One-Way Ticket

The day I flew to Thailand, I recorded a video.

I said I was ready for change. Ready for growth. Ready for the next chapter.

Then I told the truth.

*"I'm nervous as hell. I'm scared too."*

I posted a caption that said: "Your new life is going to cost you your old one." It sounded bold. Behind that post I genuinely thought I might be losing my mind.

I had put my union tool bag in storage that morning. Construction work. Steady job. Good benefits. I took a picture of it before I closed the door. That picture still sits with me.

Was I throwing away stability? Was I romanticizing escape? Was I about to embarrass myself in front of everyone who knew me?

I boarded the plane anyway.

The first two weeks weren't glamorous. Three different hotels. Dragging a suitcase through streets I didn't know. Stretching money I didn't have much of. One afternoon the power went out in the hotel for almost two hours. I sat there in the dark thinking: *what did you just do?*

My pops had given me a headlamp when I was packing. I almost didn't bring it. I was glad I did.

Sometimes you don't need the whole staircase lit. You just need enough light for the next step.

I wasn't trying to escape. I was trying to find out if the thing that once made me feel alive still had life in it. If I still had life in it.

The answer was yes. But it didn't show up immediately. It showed up slowly — in the music I started hearing again, in the landlord who became a real friend, in the quiet of a country where nobody knew my name and I had no performance to maintain.

I came home different. Not because Thailand fixed me. Because I finally got honest with myself when I had nowhere to hide.

Your new life is going to cost you your old one.

I meant it then. I still mean it now.

### Story 3 — What a Basement Taught Me About Open Doors

At nineteen, I walked into a barbershop on the Lower East Side with a dream and not much else.

I asked the owner — a man named Al — if I could rent his basement.

He didn't know me. Not really. I was a teenager with a vision and no proof that it would work. I was building my name as a mixtape DJ, putting in the work, but I wasn't there yet.

He said yes anyway.

That basement became the foundation of something. My DJ partner and I built the energy, the neighborhood brought the culture, and that combination created something you can't manufacture. Real momentum. Real community.

We sold close to 200,000 CDs out of that space.

Built a brand. Built a name. Built relationships that still stand today.

None of it happens without Al opening that door.

He didn't give me money. Didn't give me a mentor speech. He just gave me a space to let the fire breathe. And that was enough to change the trajectory of my life.

I think about Al a lot when I'm in a position to say yes to someone else. When someone younger comes to me with something unproven. When the easy call would be to pass.

An open door doesn't have to be grand. It doesn't have to cost much. Sometimes it's just a basement. Just a yes. Just someone saying: I see something in you, go ahead.

What started as a business transaction with Al became a real relationship. One that lasted long after that chapter ended.

That's what happens when someone genuinely believes in you.

Someone did that for me. It's my turn to do it for the next person. And someday — it'll be yours to pass on.

### Story 4 — Love Isn't Loud

I didn't get to raise my son the way I once dreamed.

Life had its own blueprint.

When he was still young, I was three and a half hours away. Every pickup was a 14-hour round trip. Door to door. By myself. Weekend after weekend.

Most people heard that and said: *that's too far.*

I heard it and thought: *not far enough.*

Here's what I know about love — the real kind, not the kind that makes for a good post: it's mostly quiet. It's mostly inconvenient. It shows up in things that don't get documented.

It's the drive you make even when you're tired.
It's the Target run before the weekend starts because you want him to have something to look forward to.
It's teaching him to tell time on a highway in Florida because you've got hours and he's got questions.
It's turning Marc Anthony all the way up and watching a little voice in the backseat finally let go.

The bond we have wasn't built in some perfect moment. It was built mile by mile.

I wrote this book because I wanted him to know — no matter what happened, no matter the distance or the circumstances or the times I wasn't right there — I was always with him. Every decision I made had his name tucked inside it.

Love isn't loud. It's consistent.

And I'd do it all again without hesitation.

### Story 5 — I Almost Didn't Make That Call

Something told me to check on him.

I hadn't talked to this friend in a while. No reason. Just life, distance, the noise of keeping things moving. But something nudged me. Pick up the phone. Check in.

I almost talked myself out of it. He's probably fine. I'll reach out later.

I didn't listen to that voice. I dialed.

What I found on the other end stopped me cold. He was in a dark place. A place where he was thinking about not being here anymore.

That call mattered more than either of us could have known before I dialed.

I think about that a lot. What if I'd said — *nah, he's probably good. I'll get to it.*

Later isn't promised.

I've made it a point most of my life to call people just to check in. No occasion. No reason. Just — *I was thinking about you.* It sounds small. But I've picked up the phone on random days and found people on the other end holding back tears. Carrying something they hadn't told anyone. And that one call, that short message, was enough to help them breathe again.

Your words might be the reason someone decides to stay.

Don't let doubt or busyness or pride talk you out of reaching out.

Send the message. Make the call. You don't have to have anything figured out to do it.

Just say: *I was thinking about you.*

That's enough.

### Story 6 — The Woman Who Saved Me First

Before I became the person who could write this book, someone had to pull me out.

There was a woman in my life who saw something in me worth saving — even when I was making it difficult. She came into my world when I was living two lives. From the outside, everything looked fine. The music, the hustle, the lifestyle. But underneath, I was deep in things I never should have been.

When she found out, she didn't run. She gave me an ultimatum.

Leave that life alone — or she couldn't stay.

I chose her. I chose better.

And then I spent years repaying that grace by doing all the wrong things. Lying. Living one way while pretending to be another.

The truth was I wasn't happy with myself.

But I couldn't see that yet. So instead of facing it, I ran from it — and she paid the price for wounds that were never hers to carry.

When I eventually apologized, it helped. But the feeling of knowing I let her down — knowing she didn't deserve any of it — that hasn't fully gone away. I don't think it ever will.

And maybe it shouldn't.

Some things are meant to stay with you. Not to punish you, but to keep you honest.

You can't pour real love into someone else when you haven't made peace with yourself. The unhappiness I was running from had nothing to do with her.

It was always inside me. And until I was willing to face that, I was going to keep hurting people who didn't deserve it.

Do the work. Before you bring someone else into it. Not after.

### Story 7 — The Day I Almost Stayed in the Truck

I've tried the post office.

I had a route. Steady work. Good benefits. My body sore from the day before.

Every morning I would sit in that truck before I started — and I was already tired. Not physically. Tired in a way that sleep doesn't fix. The kind of tired that comes from doing something your soul isn't in.

I've tried the sales jobs. I've tried to fit myself into shapes that were never made for me.

And every single time — I felt it. That dead feeling. Like I was living someone else's life in someone else's skin.

There was a period when I questioned everything. Including whether I even wanted to keep going.

That restless feeling — the one I used to think was a flaw, a character defect, something to fix — turned out to be a compass.

What pulled me back wasn't a plan. It was a question.

*Am I living the life I actually want?*

Not what's responsible. Not what looks right from the outside. What genuinely makes me feel alive.

Curiosity didn't just point me in a better direction. It saved my life.

Ask the question. Keep asking it. Even when the answer changes — especially when the answer changes.

The answer changing isn't failure. It's honesty.

### Story 8 — What Three Marathons Taught Me About Keeping Your Word

In 2011, I started running.

No fancy reason. I wanted a positive habit that didn't cost much. Something I could do on my own.

But something happened out on those roads that I didn't expect.

Running became more than exercise. It became structure. It became meditation. It became the one place where I could block out the noise and face myself without distraction.

I started running with a local group coached by a close friend. Every Wednesday and Saturday — I showed up. No excuses. No drama. Just people meeting up to put miles behind them.

Being around that kind of consistency forces something out of you.

It gave me a safe place to rebuild my relationship with myself. No spotlight. No validation. No identity attached to it. Just the quiet discipline of putting one foot in front of the other.

Three marathons and close to twenty half-marathons later — running still reminds me of the same thing.

Keeping your word to yourself is how you become the man you're meant to be.

Every time you skip the commitment you made to yourself, a small fracture forms inside. Your inner voice starts to hesitate. Your self-belief wavers.

And once you stop trusting yourself, everything else starts to feel out of reach.

Start small. Make a commitment. Keep it. Then make another one.

That's not a fitness tip. That's a life principle.

### Story 9 — What I Found in the Dark

The pandemic stopped the world.

For me, it started something.

By then I had already walked away from music. That chapter had closed. The world went quiet — and in that quiet, I realized I was still searching for something I couldn't name.

Then I met him.

Everything changed in that moment. And everything got more complicated at the same time.

I became a father. A real one. Present. Accountable. With something to actually live for.

But I also came face to face with every part of myself I hadn't dealt with. Every pattern. Every wound. Every thing I'd been too busy or too proud or too distracted to look at.

Becoming a father doesn't fix you. It reveals you.

It shows you every crack. Every shortcut you've been taking. Every place where you've been performing instead of being.

And then it asks: *what are you going to do about it?*

I started doing something about it.

Slowly. Imperfectly. With a lot of backtracking. But I started.

This book is part of that. It's not a victory lap. It's a report from the middle. From a man still doing the work — who wanted his son to have something real while the work is still happening.

Because I don't know how much time any of us have.

And later isn't promised.

### Story 10 — Written for One

I didn't set out to write a book for everyone.

I sat down to write a letter to my son.

I wrote it because there were things I needed him to have. Things I earned through living — through mistakes, through distance, through rebuilding myself more than once. Things I couldn't always say out loud or in the right moment.

I wrote it in case I'm not always there.

That's the part most people don't say. But it's the truest part.

Every parent carries that. The quiet fear that time isn't guaranteed. That you'll leave before you've said enough. That your kid will grow up and have questions you never got to answer.

This book is my answer to that fear.

I didn't write it to be profound. I wrote it to be present — even in my absence.

But something happened when I started writing honestly. The stories started reaching past Ethan. They started touching the places that a lot of us share but don't talk about — the fatherless son still carrying questions, the man quietly rebuilding, the parent trying to show up better than they were shown.

I wasn't just writing to him anymore.

I was writing to all of us.

That's how a letter becomes a book.

Written for one. Speaks to many.
