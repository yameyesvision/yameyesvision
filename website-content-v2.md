## SECTION 2: THE STORY

**Opening (no header — just drops in):**

I grew up on the Lower East Side of New York in the nineties. Raised mostly by my grandmother — a woman I loved deeply, who was fighting her own battles. My mom was around, doing her best. My brother got pulled into the streets early. My biological father was nowhere.

So I figured things out the way a lot of kids like me do — by watching, absorbing, and sometimes choosing wrong on purpose just to see what happened.

What I had, though, was people. My uncle, who suited up for work every single day at 8am at the bus stop — rain, snow, it didn't matter. My stepdad, who stepped into a space I didn't have a name for and gave it balance. Friends' parents who treated me like their own. Even the hustlers on the block, who saw something in me and in their own way tried to steer me away from what they'd gotten caught in.

That's fire being passed, even from imperfect hands.

**Break — new beat:**

I spent fifteen years as a DJ. Built a name, distributed close to 200,000 CDs out of a basement barbershop on the Lower East Side, played stages across the country. From the outside, it looked like I had it together.

On the inside, I was carrying weight I hadn't looked at yet.

Eventually I walked away from music. Moved to Florida. Started doing the real work — the quiet kind. I made 14-hour round trips on weekends just to spend time with my son. I moved in with a great-grandmother who cried for five minutes straight when I walked through her door because she'd been alone too long. I packed a bag, bought a one-way ticket to Thailand with more gut than money, and spent a year out there finding out if I still had something left in me.

I did.

**Break — present tense:**

I'm not a finished product. I'm not writing this from the other side of all of it. I'm writing it from the middle — a man still figuring things out, still showing up, still choosing.

But I've learned enough to know that some things need to be said out loud before it's too late.

*To the Son Who Saved Me* is that.

## SECTION 2: THE FULL DESCRIPTION

**Headline:**
Some books are written to be read.
This one was written to be felt.

**Body:**

Before Ethan could read, his father was already writing this for him.

*To the Son Who Saved Me: 10 Truths for the Road Ahead* is a raw, deeply personal letter from a father to his son — part memoir, part life manual, all heart. It's the book that gets written when a man survives himself and wants to make sure his child doesn't have to start from zero.

Growing up on the Lower East Side of New York in the nineties, Adam Llamas found himself raising a family while still being raised by life. He hustled his way to fifteen years behind the turntables, building a name, a career, and a lifestyle that looked like success from the outside. But underneath it all, he was carrying weight he hadn't dealt with — and eventually, life made him deal with it.

What followed was a decade of real reckoning: walking away from music, moving across state lines, flying solo to Thailand on a one-way ticket, rebuilding himself from scratch, and discovering that the boy who called him "Dad" for the first time over a bowl of Froot Loops had already saved his life without knowing it.

This book holds the ten truths he earned the hard way — not rules, not a manual, but hard-won wisdom from a man who has lived it, bled through it, and found his way forward.

Lessons about gratitude when life is hard.
About keeping your word even when it costs you.
About asking for help when pride says don't.
About what love looks like when you actually mean it.
About falling apart with dignity and rising back up with purpose.

Written for Ethan. Honest enough to reach anyone who has ever been lost, rebuilt themselves in silence, or loved someone more than they knew how to say.

**This is a father's promise, written in full.**

## SECTION 3: THE 10 TRUTHS — FULL

**Headline:** What's inside.

---

**01 — Gratitude Is a Light: Let It Shine**
*Don't hide your thanks. Speak it, show it, let it soften your edges.*

In the hardest years — new city, thin margins, starting over — Adam built one small habit: every time he got in the car, he'd list five things he was grateful for. Your name was always at the top. This chapter is about why that practice didn't just help him cope. It changed the direction of his life.

---

**02 — Say the Words: They Matter More Than You Know**
*Don't let pride silence your heart.*

Actions can be misread. Clear words can't. This chapter is about the phone calls that changed people's lives, the apologies that came too late, the day a little boy put down his iPad and said "Dad" like it was the most natural thing in the world — and what that did to a man who'd been quietly waiting for it.

---

**03 — Your Word Is Bond: Honor It**
*Stand on it, even when it's inconvenient.*

Your reputation walks into the room before you do. This chapter traces what integrity looks like in real life — from an uncle at a bus stop at 8am every day without fail, to three marathons and twenty half-marathons run on promises made to himself, to what it means to be the kind of man people can actually count on.

---

**04 — A True Apology Humbles and Heals**
*An apology isn't just words. It's a bridge.*

What separates boys from men isn't perfection — it's response. This chapter is about the woman who helped pull him out of a life that could have cost him everything, the ways he let her down anyway, and what he learned about accountability, ego, and what it actually means to make things right.

---

**05 — Questions Are Keys: Use Them**
*If your mind is foggy, ask. There's no shame in seeking clarity.*

This chapter is about the restlessness that wouldn't go away — the feeling of standing behind a counter at sixteen knowing he was built for something else, the fifteen years chasing it, the moment the rooms stopped feeling right, and the question that eventually saved his life: *Am I living the life I actually want?*

---

**06 — Even Mountains Lean: So Can You**
*If you're stuck, say it. Even kings need counsel.*

There is a myth sold to men for generations: that strength means carrying everything alone. This chapter is about a night in Thailand that broke something open, a sister who loaned him what he needed without asking for a guarantee, a friend who just said *I believe in you* — and what Adam learned about the difference between leaning and collapsing.

---

**07 — Extend Your Hand When You Can**
*Someone else's storm may need your shelter. Be that for them.*

Real giving doesn't come with a receipt. This chapter moves from holding people down on the Lower East Side, to an unexpected move to central Florida, to a great-grandmother who cried for five straight minutes the day he walked through her door — and what happened to both of them because he stayed.

---

**08 — Pass On the Fire, Not Just the Flame**
*What you learn, you teach. That's how legacies move through time.*

A flame keeps something alive for a moment. Fire gives it roots. This chapter goes back to a barbershop basement where 200,000 CDs were sold, a man named Al who gave a teenager an open door, and what it means to let your ceiling become someone else's floor.

---

**09 — Fall With Grace, Rise With Grit**
*Falling isn't failure. Staying down is. Get up. Every time.*

During the pandemic, the world went quiet. Adam found himself searching for something he couldn't name — until he found you. This chapter is about what it looks like to fall apart, stay honest about it, and rebuild anyway. Not for the highlight reel. For the kid watching.

# DESIGN NOTES FOR STITCH

*(Pass these to the designer or use as prompts in Google Stitch)*

**Color palette feel:**
Warm but not soft. Think early morning light, aged wood, open road. Not dark/moody, not bright/cheerful — somewhere honest in between. Earth tones. Deep greens. Warm whites. One strong accent color (burnt orange or deep gold).

**Typography feel:**
- Headline font: Something with weight and character. Serif or strong sans-serif. Not trendy.
- Body font: Clean and readable. Generous line-height. Let the words breathe.
- Pull quotes: Larger, italic, full-width. These should stop people scrolling.

**Photography direction:**
- Lead with the camping photo (backs to camera, looking up) — it IS the brand
- No photos of Ethan's face
- Author photos: real, not posed — outdoors, road, movement preferred
- Book cover prominently on Book page

**Overall feeling:**
A man handing something to his son. Quiet strength. Nothing to prove. Just honest.
