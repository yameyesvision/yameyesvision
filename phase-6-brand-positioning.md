## To the Son Who Saved Me | Adam Llamas

## PART 1: WHAT YOU STAND FOR

This isn't a brand exercise. This is clarity.

Before you can position yourself to anyone else, you need to know — with no hesitation — what this is actually about. Not what sounds good. What's true.

---

**You stand for:**

**Showing up when it would be easier not to.**
Not the dramatic, movie-moment kind of showing up. The quiet, nobody-watching, 14-hours-round-trip kind. The kind that doesn't get posted. That's your whole story — and it's the thing most people are starving to see modeled.

**Telling the truth about the hard parts.**
Not the sanitized version. Not the redemption arc with all the mess edited out. The version where you were the problem sometimes. Where you made the wrong call. Where you sat in a dark hotel room in Thailand wondering what you'd just done. That honesty is rare — and it's the thing that makes people trust you.

**Proving that falling doesn't disqualify you.**
You're not writing from a mountaintop. You're writing from the middle. Still rebuilding. Still figuring it out. Still choosing. And that's exactly what makes this credible. People don't need more perfect examples. They need someone honest about the mess.

**Legacy as a daily practice, not a final destination.**
Legacy isn't something you leave when you die. It's something you build in ordinary moments — a road trip, a chess game, a gratitude list in a car in Miami. That reframe is yours. Own it.

---

## PART 2: WHO THIS IS FOR

Be specific. Trying to reach everyone means reaching no one.

---

**Primary audience:**

**Fathers who feel the gap.**
Men who love their kids deeply but feel like they're falling short. Who show up but can't always say the words. Who are rebuilding themselves while trying to be present for someone else at the same time. They don't need more pressure. They need someone to say: *I see you, and it counts.*

---

**Secondary audiences:**

**The son who never got this.**
Grown men — twenties, thirties, forties — who never had a father hand them a blueprint. Who figured everything out alone. Who are still carrying questions nobody ever answered. This book doesn't replace what they missed. But it gives them something.

**People in a rebuild.**
Not just parents. Anyone who walked away from a life that wasn't working and is starting over in the honest, unglamorous way. They're not looking for inspiration. They're looking for proof that someone else did it and came out the other side with something real.

**Women who want to gift this.**
Mothers, partners, sisters — buying this for the man in their life who won't buy it for himself but needs it. This is a real purchase pattern for books like this. Don't ignore it.

---

## PART 3: WHAT MAKES YOUR STORY DIFFERENT

There are a lot of books about fatherhood. A lot of memoirs about rebuilding. A lot of personal development books with numbered truths.

Here's what none of them have:

**Your specific combination.**
Lower East Side in the nineties. Fifteen years as a mixtape DJ. 200,000 CDs out of a barbershop basement. Walking away from music when the rooms stopped feeling right. Moving across state lines. A 14-hour round trip. One-way ticket to Thailand. A great-grandmother who became your best friend. A son who called you Dad over a bowl of Froot Loops.

Nobody else has that story. Nobody.

**You're not writing from the finish line.**
Most memoir authors wait until they've arrived before they write. You're writing from the middle — still doing the work, still showing up, still choosing. That's not a weakness. That's the most relatable position you can be in.

**The letter format changes everything.**
This isn't advice to strangers. It's a father talking to his son. That intimacy changes how it reads — and how it lands. Readers don't feel lectured. They feel like they're overhearing something private and true.

**You didn't clean it up.**
The woman you let down. The choices you're not proud of. The nights that felt impossible. You left that in. That honesty is the differentiator. Anyone can write about lessons learned. Not everyone has the courage to show the cost of learning them.

## PART 5: LONG BIO

*(For About page, media kit, speaking engagements — 250–350 words)*

---

Adam Llamas grew up on the Lower East Side of New York City in the nineties — raised primarily by his grandmother, shaped by a neighborhood that had its own rules, and guided by a handful of people who chose to pass something real down to him before he even knew he needed it.

He found his way to music early. By nineteen, he was building his name as a mixtape DJ out of a barbershop basement on the Lower East Side. What started as a dream became fifteen years behind the turntables — close to 200,000 CDs distributed, stages and clubs across the country, a name that traveled further than he expected.

But music was always the outside story.

The inside one was harder and more honest. Navigating a childhood without a biological father. Learning what integrity meant by watching men who had it. Falling in love. Falling apart. Starting over. Doing it again.

He walked away from music when the rooms stopped feeling right. Moved to Florida. Made 14-hour round trips on weekends because that was what showing up looked like. Became the primary companion to a 90-year-old great-grandmother who cried for five minutes the day he walked through her door. Boarded a one-way flight to Thailand with no plan and not enough money — just a gut feeling and a name he kept in his chest everywhere he went.

He came back different. Not because he had everything figured out. Because he finally stopped pretending he did.

*To the Son Who Saved Me* is his first book — a letter to his son Ethan, structured around ten truths earned through real living. It is part memoir, part life manual, all heart.

Adam currently lives in Florida. He is a father, a writer, a runner, and a work in progress.

## PART 6: THREE POSITIONING ANGLES

*Use these to tailor your messaging to different platforms and audiences.*
*You don't change who you are — you choose which part of the story leads.*

---

### Angle 1 — FATHERHOOD
**Lead with:** The relationship. The drives. The Froot Loops. The bond built mile by mile.
**Who it reaches:** Fathers, parents, family-focused audiences
**Tone:** Warm, proud, present
**Where it works best:** Instagram, Facebook, parenting communities, podcasts about family

**Positioning statement:**
> "A father who couldn't always be in the room — but made sure his presence lived in every decision he made."

**Content that fits this angle:**
- The 14-hour drive videos
- The chess games
- The camping photo
- "Love isn't loud. It's consistent."
- Teaching him to tell time
- The first time he said "Dad"

---

### Angle 2 — HEALING & REBUILDING
**Lead with:** The walk away. The rebuild. Thailand. Starting over with intention.
**Who it reaches:** People in transitions, men's wellness, personal growth communities, anyone rebuilding after a hard chapter
**Tone:** Honest, grounded, no toxic positivity
**Where it works best:** TikTok, YouTube, podcasts about growth and mental health, LinkedIn

**Positioning statement:**
> "A man who had to lose the life he'd built before he could build the one that actually fit."

**Content that fits this angle:**
- The one-way ticket script
- Sitting in the post office truck already tired
- Walking away from music when the rooms stopped feeling right
- Thailand — the dark hotel room, the headlamp
- "Your new life is going to cost you your old one."
- Rebuilding yourself choice by choice

---

### Angle 3 — LEGACY & WISDOM
**Lead with:** The truths. The lessons. What gets passed down.
**Who it reaches:** Personal development audience, men seeking guidance, readers of memoir and self-help crossovers
**Tone:** Reflective, direct, earned authority
**Where it works best:** YouTube long-form, Substack, podcasts about purpose and legacy, BookTok

**Positioning statement:**
> "Not writing from a mountaintop. Writing from the middle — with ten truths that cost something to learn."

**Content that fits this angle:**
- The 10 truths as individual pieces
- "Pass on the fire, not just the flame"
- The uncle at the bus stop every morning
- Al and the basement
- Running as a promise to yourself

## PART 8: WHAT NOT TO DO

*Guardrails for staying in your lane.*

**Don't position yourself as an expert.**
You're not a parenting expert or a self-help guru. You're a man who lived through something and wrote it down honestly. That's more valuable. Protect that.

**Don't chase trends.**
The book's strength is its timelessness. A trend-chasing caption undermines the whole tone. When in doubt, go quieter, not louder.

**Don't perform vulnerability.**
There's a version of "raw and honest" content that's actually just performance. You'll feel the difference. If you're posting something because it sounds vulnerable — stop. Only post what's actually true.

**Don't separate the man from the message.**
Your personal life, your platforms, and your book are all one story. The consistency between them is your credibility. The Adam in the DMs should sound like the Adam in the book.
